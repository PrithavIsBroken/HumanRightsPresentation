Who is Samuel Moyn?

  • Professor of Law & History at Yale University.
  • The Provocateur: He challenges the "sacred cow" of Human Rights.
  • "I am not against rights. I am saying they are insufficient."

Key Vocabulary (Part 1)

Indigence

Extreme poverty. Lacking the absolute basics to survive (food, shelter).
Moyn argues we have focused entirely on fixing this.

Inequality

The gap between the rich and the poor. It's about distribution, not just survival.
Moyn argues we have ignored this.

Key Vocabulary (Part 2)

Subsistence

Maintaining oneself at a minimum level. Just staying alive.

Neoliberalism

A political approach favoring free-market capitalism, deregulation, and reduction in government spending (popular since the 1970s).

Egalitarianism

The doctrine that all people are equal and deserve equal rights and opportunities—specifically regarding wealth.

Symbols & Metaphors

King Croesus

The benevolent dictator. Represents a system that gives charity but keeps all power.

The Floor

Human Rights. A safety net to prevent you from hitting rock bottom.

The Ceiling

The limit on wealth. Moyn says this is missing from our society.

The Great Paradox

Rights
Inequality

"Why has the age of Human Rights also been the age of massive Inequality?"

What are Human Rights?

Traditionally, these are protections against the State.

  • Free Speech
  • No Torture
  • Religious Freedom
Focus: STATUS (You are a human, so you are safe).

What is Inequality?

The economic hierarchy of the world.

  • Wealth Concentration
  • Lack of Social Mobility
  • Corporate Power
Focus: MATERIAL WEALTH (How much do you own?).

Class Poll

If you could only choose one guarantee, which would it be?

A. The right to say whatever I want (Free Speech)
Moyn says our world chose this one.
B. The guarantee that I will never be poor (Economic Security)
Moyn argues we forgot about this one.

NOT ENOUGH

Moyn's Thesis:
Human rights are necessary to prevent catastrophe, but they are insufficient to create a fair society because they do not address wealth distribution.

Concept 1: Sufficiency

(The Floor)

"Sufficiency" asks: Does everyone have enough to survive?

"It doesn't matter if the guy next door has a private jet, as long as you have bread."

Concept 2: Equality

(The Ceiling)

"Equality" asks: How far apart are the rich and the poor?

"It DOES matter if he has a jet, because that massive wealth gives him power over you."

The House of Justice

The Floor (Human Rights)
The Missing Ceiling (Equality)

Wealth keeps going up unchecked.

The Parable of Croesus

Imagine a King who owns everything.
He is kind. He gives every peasant food, water, and shelter.
No one is starving. No one is tortured.

Is Croesus' Kingdom Just?

Human Rights View

YES. No rights are violated. Everyone has "sufficiency." The floor is secure.

Moyn's View

NO. It is a dictatorship of wealth. Without equality (a ceiling), the people are just well-fed pets.

How did we get here?

Moyn takes us on a historical tour to show that we didn't always separate "Rights" from "Money."

PHASE 1: 1790s

The Jacobin Legacy

During the French Revolution, "Rights" didn't just mean freedom. It meant Social Welfare.

They believed the state should redistribute wealth.
Liberty and Equality were married.

PHASE 2: 1940s-1960s

The Welfare State

The "Heroic Age." Governments taxed the rich to build schools, hospitals, and roads.

  • High Taxes on the Rich
  • Strong Unions
  • Goal: Reduce the Class Gap
PHASE 3: 1970s-Present

The Neoliberal Turn

Privatization

Selling state assets to private companies.

Deregulation

Removing rules that controlled businesses.

Globalization

Markets became global; protections became weaker.

The Betrayal

Human Rights

"I will protect you from torture!"

+
Neoliberalism

"I will take all your money!"

Moyn argues Human Rights became a "powerless companion" to Neoliberalism.

"Human rights are a floor of protection, but they have failed to provide a ceiling on inequality."

- Samuel Moyn

Real World: The 1% vs 99%

  • Jeff Bezos has enough money to end world hunger, yet Amazon workers struggle.
  • This is legal. No human rights are "violated" by being a billionaire.
  • Moyn's Point: The legal framework allows extreme inequality.
NEPAL SPECIFIC

The Constitution of 2015

31 Fundamental Rights

Right to Housing, Food, Education, Health...

On paper, Nepal has one of the most progressive constitutions in the world.

The "Floor" is excellent.

But... The Reality?

Brain Drain

Thousands of youth leave for the Gulf, Australia, and USA every month. Why leave a country with so many "Rights"?

The Disconnect

Because rights on paper don't equal economic opportunity. The "Ceiling" (economic structure) is crushing the "Floor."

The "Croesus" of Kathmandu

Think about the gap between the urban elite in Kathmandu and rural farmers in Karnali.

We often see charity (giving rice/blankets) as the solution.

But charity is not Justice. Charity implies a master and a servant.

Why Laws Aren't Enough

Symptomatic Treatment

Laws punish the crime, but don't fix the poverty that caused it.

Wealth Buys Influence

In an unequal world, the rich can "buy" better justice.

Scenario: The Cooperative Crisis

A Cooperative CEO steals billions. He is arrested (Human Rights law working). But the poor lose their life savings.

Did the system work?

YES: The thief was caught.
Moyn says this is shallow justice.
NO: The structural failure destroyed lives anyway.
Correct. Prevention (Equality) is better than Cure (Punishment).

So, What is the Solution?

Keep the Floor

Don't ditch Human Rights. We need them to prevent tyranny.

Build a Ceiling

We need a "New Politics" that taxes wealth, funds public services, and limits extreme power.

What can Students do?

1. Awareness

Understand that being "charitable" isn't the same as being "fair." Don't just donate; ask why poverty exists.

2. Local Activism

Support public education and health. These are the great equalizers.

3. Question Narratives

When you see a "success story" of a billionaire, ask: "At whose cost?"

Conclusion

We cannot be satisfied with a world that is merely "not cruel."
We must strive for a world that is fair.
